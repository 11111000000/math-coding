Philosophy

statement

A convention that documents every non-trivial decision as a proof term, applied to itself.

Why

The AI-coding-agent era has a problem that commit messages cannot solve. After enough time, even a careful author cannot recall why a particular implementation was chosen. Six months later, the commit message says fix: handle null case. The proposition — what null-cases we accept, what null-cases we reject, why the line is drawn here — is gone. The agent that wrote it does not remember. The reviewer does not remember. Nobody does.

math-coding is the response: write the proposition before the implementation, in plain text, alongside the code. Five files per decision. They survive git, grep, and the test of time.

antithesis

This could be ceremony without signal. If a team creates packets for every commit — typo, dependency bump, rename — the convention becomes noise, and the packets that actually matter get lost in the volume. A convention that demands everything demands nothing.

synthesis

math-coding refuses to be ceremony. Three modes exist precisely so trivial changes can stay trivial: light writes a commit message; standard writes the full 5-file packet; strict adds theory links and surface impact. The question is not "should I create a packet?" but "is this decision-class?" The decision-class test is simple:

Would I be surprised to see this in 6 months? If yes, write a packet.

What we refuse to be

The quick summary lives on the home page. Below: what each of those means in practice.

  • Not a framework. There is no CLI to install, no runtime, no opinion about language or stack. The conventions are plain text. The agent reads them.
  • Not a test runner. The verifier checks structure, not behavior. Behavioral tests belong in your project's own CI.
  • Not a governance tool. math-coding cannot enforce anything in a project that has not adopted it. It makes promises to its own adopters only.
  • Not a shield against dishonest agents. This is the point that needs more than a bullet. The convention operates on outputs, not on the LLM's internal state. An adversarial model can mark a speculation as fact, submit --approve without reading the criteria, or skip self-critique entirely. The convention cannot detect what the agent thought — only what it wrote. Treat epistemic honesty as a protocol for honest agents: it raises the floor for those who want to do good work, it does not raise the ceiling against those who want to game it. External governance — human review, sampling audits, restricted compute — remains the only real defense.

The substrate

axiom A3 (Material Basis) declares that the convention lives in plain text, in git, and runs on POSIX shell. This is not a passing preference. Plain text (1960s), git (2005), and POSIX (1988) are older than most projects that will adopt math-coding. They will outlive it.

A convention that depends on a language, framework, IDE, or vendor outlives its usefulness only as long as that substrate survives. We refuse to make the convention a hostage of fashion. core/ scripts run on dash, ash, busybox sh — not bash, not Python, not Node.

Self-application

The seventh axiom declares that the convention applies to itself. Every axiom above is realised as a packet under math/. The verifier that checks those packets is itself the subject of a packet. sh math-coding probe exits 0 ⟺ the convention is internally consistent.

This is a strong claim. Most conventions cannot verify themselves; they rely on a maintainer's judgment, and when the maintainer leaves, the convention rots in silence. math-coding's self-application closes that loop. The convention observes its own drift. The convention records that observation.
